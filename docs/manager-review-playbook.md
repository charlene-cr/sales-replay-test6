# Manager Review Playbook

This playbook describes how a frontline manager can turn replay output into a short coaching conversation.

## Weekly Rhythm

1. Pick three calls: one won, one active follow-up, and one at-risk opportunity.
2. Read the summary before the one-on-one so the meeting starts with evidence.
3. Ask the rep to explain what they heard from the buyer before offering advice.
4. End with a specific next-step behavior that can be checked in the next replay.

## Review Questions

- What business pain did the buyer say out loud?
- Did the rep ask a second question before presenting the product?
- Which objection was answered with evidence instead of reassurance?
- Was the next step tied to a date, owner, and decision artifact?
- What should the rep write differently in the follow-up email?

## Signals To Watch

- Security questions without a named security reviewer.
- Procurement blockers mentioned after a verbal yes.
- Budget timing without a reconnect trigger.
- Pilots that measure activity but not business impact.
- Follow-up meetings scheduled without a concrete agenda.

## Coaching Examples

- Strong discovery: the rep asks what changed and where time would be reinvested.
- Weak discovery: the rep jumps to implementation details before confirming pain.
- Strong objection handling: the rep names the concern and offers a specific artifact.
- Weak objection handling: the rep says the team can help but does not prove it.
- Strong next step: the buyer commits to a reviewer, date, and packet.

## Common Review Mistakes

- Reviewing too many calls and leaving without one behavior change.
- Treating a procurement blocker as a product objection.
- Accepting a calendar invite as a next step when no artifact is named.
- Coaching the outcome instead of the buyer language that produced it.
- Skipping won calls, even though they contain repeatable team habits.

## Sample One-On-One Agenda

1. Rep summarizes the buyer situation in two minutes.
2. Manager reads the most relevant transcript excerpt.
3. Rep names the objection they heard and how they answered it.
4. Manager picks one behavior to repeat or change.
5. Rep commits to the next artifact the manager will inspect.

## Before The Meeting

- Read the replay summary and pick one call to discuss.
- Mark the buyer phrase that best explains urgency.
- Check whether the next step has a date and owner.
- Bring one example of strong language from another rep.
- Decide whether the conversation is about discovery, objection handling, or follow-up writing.

## After The Meeting

- Save the behavior change in the rep's coaching notes.
- Inspect the next follow-up email for the agreed behavior.
- Re-score the next comparable call before the next one-on-one.
- Share a positive example in team review when the behavior improves.
- Remove stale follow-up tasks when the buyer has gone quiet.

## Example Coaching Notes

- Maya: repeat the security packet framing from Northstar when procurement joins late.
- Jon: slow down in early discovery and ask one more workflow-impact question.
- Priya: keep the technical readout agenda tied to director approval.
- Sam: write reconnect triggers directly into the follow-up note.
- Team: use buyer language in summaries rather than internal product shorthand.

## Escalation Rules

- Escalate if legal asks about data retention and no answer exists.
- Escalate if procurement requests a packet and the deal is forecasted for the current month.
- Escalate if a security reviewer is unnamed after two calls.
- Escalate if the pilot has success metrics but no production owner.
- Escalate if a manager cannot identify the next artifact from the transcript.

## Definition Of Done

- The rep can quote the buyer's stated pain.
- The manager can name the behavior being coached.
- The next artifact is visible before the next one-on-one.
